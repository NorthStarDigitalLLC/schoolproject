The Trial of Victor Frankenstein
COURT OF MORAL JUSTICE · 1818

The Trial of
Victor Frankenstein

Victor Frankenstein is guilty because he creates life and then abandons it, and his failure to take responsibility directly causes the Creature's suffering and the deaths that follow.

Charges

Victor Frankenstein stands accused on three counts:

  1. Negligence as a creator.
  2. Willful abandonment.
  3. Indirect responsibility for the deaths of William, Clerval, and Elizabeth.
CONTEXT

Background of the Case

Victor Frankenstein is a scientist who discovers how to create life. After bringing the Creature to life, he is horrified by it and quickly rejects and abandons responsibility for it. The Creature, left alone with no guidance, must learn about the world on its own and is repeatedly rejected by society.

They follow from the suffering Victor's abandonment and rejection caused. The prosecution argues this reveals a clear chain of cause and effect.

CROSS-TEXT CONNECTIONS

Identity Is Shaped by Environment

The idea that identity is shaped by external forces is not unique to Frankenstein.

EDUCATED
Tara Westover

Shaped by Isolation

Like the Creature, Tara is shaped by her environment and upbringing. She grows up in isolation without education or outside influence. Her identity is shaped by neglect and control from her father — but unlike the Creature, she is eventually able to break free through education. This shows that environment shapes identity powerfully, but the path out depends on the support a person is given.

1984
George Orwell

Shaped by Power

Like the Creature, Winston is shaped and controlled by external forces. The Party manipulates his beliefs, controls truth, and dictates reality. Winston tries to resist, but the Party's power ultimately reshapes him. This shows how environment and power structures can overwhelm even those who fight back — just as the Creature is shaped by Victor and society.

Across all three texts, individuals do not develop in isolation — they are influenced by their environment. This strengthens the argument that Victor is responsible, because he created the conditions that shaped the Creature's actions.

FOR THE DEFENSE

The Defense Argues

The defense argues that the Creature is responsible for his own actions because he demonstrates clear intelligence, moral awareness, and the ability to make choices. Throughout the novel, the Creature learns language, reads books, and understands human emotions and morality. This shows that he knows the difference between right and wrong.

Despite this understanding, he chooses violence. After being rejected, he does not seek peaceful solutions but instead turns to revenge. He deliberately murders William, frames Justine, and later kills Clerval and Elizabeth. These are not accidental actions — they are planned and intentional.

Additionally, the Creature himself admits control over his actions when he tells Victor, "I will be with you on your wedding-night." This shows premeditation and awareness. Because he knowingly chooses revenge, the defense argues that responsibility lies with the Creature, not Victor.

This same principle appears in Educated and 1984: Tara eventually chooses to leave and educate herself, and Winston attempts to resist the Party. Both show that individuals can make choices despite their environment. The Creature, like them, had the ability to choose — meaning responsibility may still lie with him.

FOR THE PROSECUTION

The Prosecution Responds

Victor Frankenstein is responsible because he created life and failed to take responsibility for it, directly causing the Creature's suffering and actions.

The Creature was not born violent. He states, "I was benevolent and good; misery made me a fiend." This proves his nature was originally kind. His transformation into violence is the result of his experiences, not his creation.

Victor quickly rejects the Creature and abandons responsibility after bringing it to life, feeling "horror and disgust." Instead of guiding or teaching his creation, he leaves it alone in a world it does not understand. As the Creature tries to connect with others, he is met with violence — when Felix attacks him, the rejection causes emotional suffering and isolation, which leads to his anger.

This pattern is not unique to Frankenstein. In Educated, Tara's identity is shaped by isolation and her father's control. In 1984, Winston is shaped by the Party. Across all three texts, individuals are formed by their environment — proving the Creature's transformation followed a recognizable pattern Victor created.

Victor is given a second chance when the Creature asks for a companion, but he destroys the second creation. He is aware of the consequences, yet still refuses to act responsibly. The prosecution argues that the deaths that follow are not random — they are the result of Victor's neglect. This is a clear chain of cause and effect.

CLOSING STATEMENT · FOR THE PROSECUTION

Victor Frankenstein is guilty because he created life but refused responsibility for it.

The Creature was not born evil, but shaped by neglect and rejection.

Victor had the power to guide his creation but failed to do so.

This chain of cause and effect shows that Victor's actions directly caused the outcome.

Victor had power, but failed to take responsibility for it.

THE COURT OF MORAL JUSTICE FINDS
The defendant, Victor Frankenstein —
GUILTY

Victor Frankenstein is guilty on all counts because his actions directly caused the outcome.
